# Symptom framing — how clinicians want to hear it

Doctors triage a symptom along a small number of dimensions. Describe your problem along these lines and you've handed them the exact pattern they're listening for — in the first minute. The standard frame is **OPQRST**.

| Letter | Means | Plain-language prompt | Example answer |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **O** | Onset | When did it start? Sudden or gradual? What were you doing? | "Started three weeks ago, came on slowly." |
| **P** | Provocation / Palliation | What makes it worse? What makes it better? | "Worse on stairs, better when I rest." |
| **Q** | Quality | What does it feel like, in your own words? | "A tightness, not a sharp pain." |
| **R** | Region / Radiation | Where is it? Does it travel anywhere? | "Center of the chest, doesn't move." |
| **S** | Severity | How bad, 0–10? Does it stop you doing things? | "About a 5, makes me stop and sit." |
| **T** | Time | How long does it last? How often? Changing over time? | "A few minutes each time, a few times a week, steady so far." |

## How to use it
- You don't need every letter for every problem. Use what fits.
- For things that aren't pain — fatigue, a rash, dizziness, low mood — the same shape still works: when it started, what makes it better or worse, what it's like, where, how much it affects you, and how it's changed.
- "How it's changed over time" is the single most useful and most forgotten piece. Getting worse, better, or staying steady tells a clinician a lot.

## The one-sentence version
If you prepare only one sentence, make it this shape:

> "For **[how long]**, I've had **[what]** that's **[where]**, made worse by **[trigger]** and better by **[relief]**, and it's been **[getting worse / better / steady]**."
